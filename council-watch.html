SB 79 — Palo Alto's response

Status: Deferred from May 4, 2026; did not return on May 18 (that meeting covered other business). Now scheduled for June 1, 2026 as Item 17. Staff and the Council's SB 79 Ad Hoc Committee recommend adopting a historic-resource exemption ordinance and a 50% rezone ordinance, with interim urgency versions on June 15.

What's at stake, simply

SB 79 is going to allow taller, denser apartment buildings around Palo Alto's three Caltrain stations. That part isn't up for debate. The choice Council faces is whether to do anything about it — and the difference between those choices is bigger than it looks.

If Council does nothing, the new state minimums kick in on their own on July 1, 2026 — 95 feet (about 9–10 stories) right next to each station, 75 feet (about 7 stories) within a quarter-mile walk, 65 feet (about 5–6 stories) within a half-mile walk. No Council vote, no city law, no staff work needed.

If Council wants to push back, SB 79 itself provides fast off-ramps. The city can adopt a local ordinance — before July 1 — that exempts historic sites and/or rezones the transit parcels to 50% of SB 79's capacity; those parcels are then excluded from the state minimums until about 2032. This does not need state sign-off and is not a multi-year project — it is a city ordinance that can be adopted in weeks. (A full alternative plan, reviewed and approved by the state, is a separate and much bigger option.)

Either way, by around 2032 SB 79 applies in full at all qualifying sites near the three Caltrain stations — unless the city has adopted a longer-term alternative plan by then. The near-term choice is about how much gets built before 2032 and who writes the rules: the state's uniform minimums, or the city's own ordinance.

Separately, Council can also vote to do other things: allow taller buildings on city-owned land, write design rules, update area plans. Each of those needs its own Council vote and its own staff work. None happen by default.

What staff is proposing — June 1, Item 17

The SB 79 item returns to Council on June 1, 2026 as Item 17, after being bumped from March 9 and then May 4. Staff and the Council's SB 79 / Downtown Housing Plan Ad Hoc Committee (Councilmembers Burt and Lauing) recommend adopting two of the four options below at once — B and C:

  • A historic-resource exemption ordinance (Option B) — excludes locally-designated historic resources from SB 79's standards.
  • A "50% SB 79 development standards" ordinance (Option C) — creates a new "TOD Combining District" that rezones every transit parcel (except historic ones) to half of SB 79's floor-area capacity, and excludes those parcels from the state minimums until roughly 2032.

Each is introduced as a temporary ordinance on June 1 (first reading), with a matching interim urgency ordinance adopted June 15. An urgency ordinance takes effect immediately, so both are in force before SB 79's July 1 start date — meaning the state's full minimums never apply to those parcels. Permanent ordinances follow later in the year through the Planning & Transportation Commission.

We verified this against the statute (California Government Code §§65912.157, 65912.160, 65912.161). The 50% exclusion is real and self-executing: once the city adopts the ordinance, SB 79's standards "shall not apply" to the covered parcels. HCD — the state housing department — receives the draft 14 days ahead and reviews the adopted ordinance within 90 days after the fact. That is notice and review, not prior approval. Only a full alternative plan (Option D) needs HCD sign-off.

What the "50%" ordinance actually does

"50%" is not a uniform half-everything cut. The draft "TOD Combining District" ordinance (a new Palo Alto Municipal Code §18.14.070) sets just one standard at half of SB 79 — floor area ratio:

Floor area ratio — within ¼ mile3.51.75 (half)
Floor area ratio — ¼ to ½ mile3.01.5 (half)

Density120 / 100 / 160 homes per acreNo cap

So under the ordinance, floor area is halved (the real binding constraint), there is no density cap at all (unit count is limited only by what fits in the floor area), and height reverts to Palo Alto's base zoning — SB 79's 65–95 ft floors stop applying. A daylight plane (starting 16 ft up at the property line, rising at 45°) protects neighbors wherever a transit parcel abuts a low-density residential district. The ordinance applies the same FAR rule to every transit parcel; it does not move density between sites the way a full alternative plan (Option D) could.

The four ways Council can respond

OptionWhat it doesWhat Council has to do
A. State minimums kick in automatically On July 1, 2026, the state's required minimum apartment sizes apply at every qualifying site near the three stations. Council can pass a city law about details (parking, design review, the approval process) but the size minimums apply either way. Nothing. Happens by default. The detail-level city law is optional housekeeping.
B. Exempt historic properties Pull listed historic sites out of the new state minimums. Limit: at most 10% of the affected area in each station's half-mile zone can be exempt this way. A Council vote on a city law, before July 1, 2026. If not passed by then, historic sites aren't protected on day one.
C. Rezone transit parcels to 50% of SB 79 (delays the state minimums) The city rezones every transit parcel to half of SB 79's floor-area capacity (the "TOD Combining District"). Those parcels are then excluded from the full state minimums until about 2032. This is what staff recommends for June 1, paired with B. A city ordinance — fast. Introduced June 1, interim urgency version adopted June 15, in force before July 1. Self-executing — no state sign-off needed; HCD reviews it after the fact. A permanent ordinance follows later through the Planning & Transportation Commission.
D. City submits a full alternative plan to the state A more ambitious version of C: the city writes a plan that redistributes where the new homes go around the stations. The state has to approve it. Total homes still have to add up to what SB 79 requires; any single station's area can be cut by at most 50%. The biggest project of all four — and the one that genuinely takes years. Needs several hundred thousand dollars of consultant work, plus HCD review and approval. Until it is adopted, SB 79's state minimums apply in the interim (unless paired with B or C). Not currently recommended by staff.

A is what happens by default. B, C, and D are each separate things Council can choose to do on top of (or instead of) A — and each requires its own vote and its own staff project. They stack: Council can pick more than one. Background — the four options in full detail.

Pro-housing advocacy · filed 2026-04-30

Palo Alto Forward

Letter from Jeremy Levine, Executive Director, filed four days before the May 4 meeting. Read the letter →

Supports Council either letting SB 79 take effect as written or adopting a full alternative plan that moves density around within the station areas. Endorses pairing the alternative plan with a fast-track historic-property ordinance as an interim bridge if the full plan takes time to pass. Explicitly opposes the half-now, rest-later option.

Their labelSite optionTheir grade
Approach 1 (No Action)A — state minimums kick inAcceptable; second-best
Approach 2 (Emergency Ordinance)B — exempt historic propertiesUseful interim companion
Approach 3 (50% Now, Rest in 2032)C — city writes its own zoning to delayOpposed — worst of both worlds
Approach 4 (TOD Alternative Plan Now)D — full alternative planRecommended

Key arguments:

  • An alternative plan lets the city move density off R-1 residential blocks onto downtown / Cal Ave / University Station sites that are better suited for it.
  • The half-now path (Option C) creates a long interim where new buildings are jarringly shorter than what their lots will support post-2032 — bad aesthetic and structural outcome.
  • Palo Alto is far behind its state housing target: only 173 units permitted in 2025, against an average of 1,092 per year needed.
  • Tradeoff they acknowledge: adopting a full alternative plan forfeits SB 79's State Density Bonus Law height exemption — but local control over where density goes outweighs that.

Note: staff's June 1 recommendation — the 50% rezone (Option C), paired with the historic exemption (Option B) — is the path this letter explicitly opposed. Palo Alto Forward argued for a full alternative plan (Option D) instead.

What to watch for on June 1

  • Does Council pass both temporary ordinances on first reading and commit to the June 15 urgency adoption? Missing July 1 opens a gap where the full state minimums apply.
  • Does Council follow the Ad Hoc Committee's B+C recommendation, or change the mix — for example, directing a full alternative plan (Option D) instead of or alongside the 50% rezone?
  • The 50% ordinance sets floor area ratio at exactly 50% of SB 79, with no cushion. Does Council direct any change to that number, or to the daylight-plane and setback standards?
  • What happens to the Downtown Housing Plan — resumed and completed (the Ad Hoc's pick), refocused on design standards, or discontinued?
  • Does any HCD feedback — the draft goes to the state 14 days ahead — surface a compliance concern before adoption?

How to engage

Council decisions get shaped by who shows up — in person, online, or in writing. Three ways in:

Live public comment

Council meets at City Hall (250 Hamilton Ave) and on Zoom. Each speaker gets up to 3 minutes (subject to the Mayor's discretion when many people sign up). Sign up before the item is called.

Meeting links & Zoom dial-in are on the agenda

Written comment

Email city.council@cityofpaloalto.org. Comments are entered into the public record and circulated to all council members. Send at least 24 hours before the meeting to ensure they're reviewed by the time of vote.

Track agendas

Council packets are posted ~11 days before each meeting on the city clerk page and on the PrimeGov portal. We list the SB 79-relevant ones above as they come up.

Why this page exists

Council decisions on SB 79 (and the wider housing conversation) move fast and aren't well-summarized in any one place. Staff reports run long; press coverage is uneven; agendas are written for procedural completeness, not for residents who want to know what's about to happen. This page is the plain-English, primary-source-grounded version. Each entry stays up until the decision lands; then it moves to a "past decisions" log (not yet present — nothing has resolved yet).
