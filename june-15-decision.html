June 15 — Council split the SB 79 ordinances

On June 15, 2026, Palo Alto City Council gave final approval to its SB 79 rules — but treated the two rules very differently on timing. It made the historic-building exemption take effect immediately, yet declined to fast-track the half-size rule the same way. As a result, for two weeks (July 1–15) full-strength SB 79 applied near the stations, and several builders filed projects to use the window. This page is the record of what Council actually did, verified against the meeting video.

The one-sentence version: the exemption for historic buildings went into force on July 1; the half-size rule for everything else did not take effect until July 16 — because Council would not make the “emergency” legal finding an urgency ordinance requires.

What Council actually did

The item was SB 79 implementation, listed as Item 23. At the meeting the City Attorney split it into three parts, and the three did not all pass:

Part What it was Result Takes effect
23A The two temporary ordinances from the June 1 first reading — the historic exemption and the half-size TOD Combining District — up for their second and final reading. Adopted July 16, 2026 (after the normal 30-day wait)
23B An urgency version of the historic exemption — same content, but immediate effect. Adopted Immediately (~July 1, 2026)
23C An urgency version of the half-size rule, applying the 50% reduction citywide with immediate effect. Did not pass — never reached a motion to adopt Never took effect; the 50% rule instead waited for 23A on July 16

Councilmembers Keith Reckdahl and George Lu were recused from the SB 79 item — each owns property inside a station's half-mile radius — leaving five members eligible to vote. Council also directed staff, on a separate motion, to study allowing more housing along specific corridors, especially California Avenue and El Camino Real, and report back within 6–12 months.

Why the half-size urgency ordinance failed

An urgency ordinance is deliberately hard to pass. It needs a four-fifths supermajority, and it requires the council to formally find “a current and immediate threat to the public health, safety or welfare.” Three of the five eligible members would not make that finding — more than enough to block it — so no one ever moved to adopt 23C. It simply died, and Council formally directed staff not to proceed with it.

The reasons the opponents gave, from the meeting:

Vice Mayor Greer Stone pointed to a contradiction in the city's own paperwork: the findings declare an “immediate threat,” but the very next sentence “acknowledges that there are no pending project applications that would be affected by the ordinance… these findings are just difficult to reconcile.” He supported the underlying policy but not the emergency framing.

Mayor Vicki Veenker weighed the legal exposure against the size of the window: “there's pretty low risk that we're going to get a slew of applications in that short time… our legal risk outweighs the risk associated with having one or maybe a couple of applications that get filed in that short window.” She also warned that claiming an emergency could jeopardize the city's pending state “prohousing” designation.

Councilmember Julie Lythcott-Haims said she had “found no evidence… that would allow me to make such a finding,” and that making emergency findings would make it “highly doubtful we'll get our prohousing designation that we just approved” earlier the same night.

A note on the vote count

Two outlets recapped June 15 (Palo Alto Online, the Daily Post) and got the headline right — the half-size urgency measure failed and a two-week gap opened. But both described it as a 3–2 vote with “Burt and Lauing in favor.” The video doesn't support that framing: no member voted to adopt the urgency ordinance — it never got a motion because the four-fifths supermajority was out of reach. The votes that were actually recorded were on a substitute motion to not proceed (5–0) and to direct staff toward a Cal Ave / El Camino study (4–1, with Councilmember Pat Burt the lone no on that staff direction). When a preview and the record diverge, we go to the record.

The two-week gap, and what happened next

Because the historic exemption (23B) took effect immediately but the half-size rule (23C) did not, the situation from July 1 to July 15 was: historic sites protected, but every other transit-adjacent parcel exposed to the full SB 79 minimums — roughly 7–10 stories near the stations — with no local reduction. The half-size rule, capping new buildings near the stations at half of SB 79's floor area, closed that gap when it took effect July 16.

Developers noticed. By early July at least three builders had filed projects to use the window, and a sixth SB 79 project — a seven-story building proposed at 135 University Avenue, within a quarter-mile of the Palo Alto Caltrain station — had been submitted by July 10. Whether these projects actually get built is a separate, multi-year question, but the filings are the concrete consequence of the choice Council made on June 15.
