﻿April 30, 2026


Palo Alto City Council
250 Hamilton Avenue
Palo Alto, CA 94301


RE: Agenda Item 14, Implementation of SB 79


To the Palo Alto City Council,
  

Palo Alto Forward works to expand housing and transportation choices in our community. In furthering our mission, we want the city to implement SB 79 in a way that maximizes housing options near transportation in our community, promoting housing affordability, walkability, transit access. 


To that end, we support the city implementing SB 79 as written, as we believe every neighborhood–especially those near transit–can benefit from additional housing options. 


However, we understand the city has additional goals. To best achieve those goals, we would also support the city implementing a good faith local alternative plan (Approach 4) to comply with SB 79. A local alternative plan will allow the city to focus new homes in the places that make the most sense near transit, jobs, and amenities. SB 79 provides a tool to further opportunities for transit-oriented homes such as the Downtown Housing Plan, the University Station Transit Center, and the Cal Ave corridor. 


By implementing a local alternative plan, Palo Alto can maximize housing allowed in the places that make the most sense for development. It can build upon its housing element commitments to actually meet its housing goals. And it can facilitate sustainable lifestyles for current and future Palo Alto residents, who will benefit from more vibrant mixed-use residential and commercial areas with more customers and more diversity of housing options. If a local alternative plan will take substantial time to pass, we would endorse allowing SB 79 to take effect combined with an emergency ordinance as proposed by staff to exempt historic sites in the interim. 


We have identified several factors that we believe some council members care about—though they are not our top priority—on which the four approaches presented in the staff report create tradeoffs. These are presented below in Table 1 on the following page, and will be explored in detail in the rest of this document. As discussed in the following pages, Approach 4 (move forward with a TOD Alternative Plan without delay) best meets the city’s specified goals for SB 79 implementation.

Analysis of Factors
The following sections explain in detail our assessments in Table 1.
Minimizes Expense to the City
Approach 1 (No Action) is absolutely free, which is categorically different from all the other approaches which would require new workstreams (including staffing, consultant services, technical report preparation, etc.) by city staff overseen by elected and appointed officials.  Accordingly, we awarded it the only green checkmark.


Approaches 2 (Ordinance) and 4 (TOD Alternative Plan) both require new workstreams.  


Approach 3 (50% now) sets itself in a different category from 2 and 4, because it requires all of the work of a TOD Alternative Plan (albeit due in 2032), and a separate comparable workstream due ASAP.  Approach 3 will involve multiple rezonings, multiple rounds of CEQA review, and HCD oversight.  Accordingly, Approach 3 is assigned the ‘red x’ for cost.


Returning to Approaches 2 and 4, we do expect 4 would be qualitatively more expensive.
Reduces R-1 Impact
Approaches 1 (No Action) + 2 (Ordinance) do not allow the city to move density around within the TOD area.  Accordingly, R-1 neighborhoods would generally be upzoned to levels consistent with midrise construction.  We have scored these options ‘red’.


Approaches 3 + 4 do allow the city to exempt R-1 areas from 50% (but not all) of the effects of SB-79 which is the most the law allows, so we have scored them both ‘green’.  We will likely see townhomes in R-1 neighborhoods even with these measures–the sort of gradual neighborhood change we hope the council will support in neighborhoods so close to transit.


Promotes Cohesive Massing
Approaches 1 (No Action), 2 (Ordinance), and 4 (TOD Alternative Plan) all move the city as quickly as possible into the land use regime that will ultimately prevail for the foreseeable future.  We believe this quality is what will lead to the best aesthetic outcomes as our downtowns evolve.


Approach 3 (50% now), in contrast, will introduce an interim period where only townhomes and low-density apartments are allowed even on sites that, after 2032, could support up to four times that density (i.e., in the event of a TOD Alternative Plan that moves the maximum allowable density from an R-1 neighborhood into a transit-adjacent downtown site.)  


Once development like that happens, it will take decades for the use to convert again during which time adjacent structure heights will be jarringly uneven. Accordingly, we have awarded Approaches 1, 2, and 4 green checks, and Approach 3 a red x.
Maximizes Housing on Best Sites 
SB-79 aligns transit infrastructure with train stops, but not with other resources such as bus routes, jobs, retail, and services.  We believe the city will want to align housing production not just with rail transit infrastructure, but also factors such as these.


Approaches 1 (No Action) and 2 (Ordinance) do a decent job of this by allowing midrise construction in such areas in the near term, but are blunt instruments.  We have scored them a “yellow checkmark.”  We also acknowledge the Ordinance offers an advantage over No Action.


Approach 3 (50% Now, Rest Later) has the disadvantage that it will zone our best sites to just 25% of their future capacity.  This risks generating actual projects at this density before 2032.  We have scored this outcome red.


Approach 4 (TOD Alternative Plan) improves upon the approaches above by allowing the city to move density from less favorable sites to more favorable sites –also in the short term.  Consequently, we have awarded this approach a green checkmark.
Maximizes Chances of Building RHNA
According to the city’s latest Annual Progress Report to Housing and Community Development, the city is not on-pace to build its sixth-cycle RHNA, having permitted just 173 units in 2025, down from 181 in 2024 and 271 in 2023. 


Going forward, the city needs to produce an average of 1,092 units per year to meet aggregate RHNA, which understates the challenge once the distribution of units across income categories is considered.


Approaches 1 (No Action) and 4 (TOD Alternative Plan) urgently move the city toward legalizing apartment-scale construction in SB-79 areas.  We have scored these approaches ‘green’.


Approach 2 (Ordinance) somewhat reduces scope of the reform, and might exclude some units. It is scored ‘yellow’.


Approach 3 (50% Now, Rest Later) unnecessarily delays (yet does not prevent) the legalization of units at this scale to as late as 2032.  It has been scored “red.”
Preserves SDBL Height Exemption
As drafted, SB-79 exempts sites in the TOD area from using State Density Bonus Law (SDBL) to exceed the heights specified in SB-79.  However, this SDBL exemption is lost if the city adopts a TOD Alternative Plan.[1]


Approaches 1 (No Action) and 2 (Ordinance) preserve this SDBL exemption, which is a local control advantage.  Without implying that this outweighs the local control advantage obtained by adopting a TOD Alternative Plan (we believe it does not), we are surfacing this factor in the table with a green checkmark.


Approaches 3 (50% Now, Rest Later) and 4 (TOD Alternative Plan) both involve forming TOD Alternative Plans and loss of SDBL exemptions, and are scored with ‘red x’.
Preserves Historic Sites
Approach 1 (No Action) is the only approach which would not likely result in historic sites being exempted from SB-79 treatment.  We have scored it as red and all others as green.
Delays or Avoids Full TOD Alternative Plan
We believe Approach 3 (50% Now, Rest Later) is presented as an approach because it defers deliberation about how to move density between areas.  As SB-79 is written, it does accomplish this.  This is not an advantage over Approach 1 (No Action) or Approach 2 (Ordinance) because those effectively defer a TOD Alternative Plan to a due date of “never,” but it is an advantage over Approach 4 (TOD Alternative Plan.)


Accordingly, we have scored Approaches 1, 2, and 3 green and Approach 4 as red for this factor.
Conclusion
A TOD Alternative Plan can best harmonize the city’s goals with the opportunity to promote housing in Palo Alto’s most desirable locations, and the city will be best served by moving toward adopting such a plan without delay.  Notably, delaying a TOD Alternative Plan for other options:
* Increases expense
* Fails to maximize our chances of meeting our housing goals
* Fails to promote cohesive build environments
* Fails to maximally align housing production with site suitability


We urge the city to either allow SB 79 to take effect as written or adopt a TOD Alternative Plan, the best way to balance housing needs with other city priorities.


Thank you for your consideration,


  

Jeremy Levine
