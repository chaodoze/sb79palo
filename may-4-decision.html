Local impact Updated 2026-05-07

The May 4, 2026 council decision, in detail

On May 4, 2026, Palo Alto City Council set the city's response to SB 79 as — in the staff briefing's own words — "a hybrid of two tracks." That phrase is meaningless without knowing what the tracks are. This page unpacks the four legal levers SB 79 actually gives a city, which ones Council picked, and what to watch over the next six years.

What Council decided

Council directed staff to pursue three things in parallel: (1) a local clarifying ordinance spelling out how SB 79 interacts with Palo Alto's existing zoning code; (2) an emergency historic-property exemption ordinance adopted before SB 79 takes effect on July 1, 2026; and (3) a rezoning effort at all three Caltrain station areas aimed at delivering at least 50% of SB 79's allowed capacity, which under the statute defers full SB 79 application until ~2032. A formal TOD Alternative Plan under Gov. Code §65912.161 was discussed but not committed to.

Council direction reported here is based on staff's pre-meeting recommendation and post-meeting press coverage. Official meeting minutes and the recorded video are pending publication on the city clerk's site — this page will be updated to quote the motion verbatim when those are posted.

The four levers SB 79 gives a city

SB 79 is not all-or-nothing. The statute itself describes several distinct ways a city can shape its response — some passive, some affirmative. They stack: a city can use more than one. Understanding them as four separate levers (rather than two undifferentiated tracks) is the only way to read what Council did.

Lever Statute What it does What it costs the city
A. Defaults + clarifying ordinance Gov. Code §65912.157 Let SB 79's height/density floors apply by default on July 1, 2026. Pass a local ordinance specifying how the law interacts with parking standards, design review, ministerial paths, and procedural details. No reduction in upzoning; no delay; predictable. Project applicants get full statutory floors.
B. Historic-property exemption Gov. Code §65912.161(a)(2)(C) Exempt sites listed on a National, California, or local historic register from SB 79's floors. Has to be done by ordinance with documented evidence of historic significance — not just "this building looks old." Capped at 10% of the eligible area in any TOD zone (the statute caps how much of a city's rezoned area can be carved out via local-register listings).
C. Rezone-to-50% delay trigger Gov. Code §65912.161(b)(1) If the city's housing-element rezoning already delivers at least 50% of SB 79's eligible capacity at the affected sites, §65912.157 (the heart of SB 79) is automatically deferred until one year after the 7th-cycle housing element is adopted — ~2032 in the Bay Area. Time, not capacity. Buys ~6 years of breathing room. The rezoning itself is a multi-year process; the city has to deliver real upzoning to claim the delay.
D. TOD Alternative Plan Gov. Code §65912.161(a) Submit a comprehensive plan to HCD that maintains the same total net zoned capacity as SB 79 would unlock, just distributed differently across the half-mile bands. HCD reviews and signs off. Plan must maintain total capacity; individual TOD zones can be cut up to 50%; site-level density capped at 200% of SB 79's chapter max. Significant staff effort and political negotiation.

Two important relationships:

  • B and C stack. A city can pass historic exemptions and trigger the rezone-to-50% delay. They aren't substitutes.
  • D is the bigger move. An Alternative Plan is more ambitious than a delay trigger — it actively redraws the upzoning map — and it's the only lever that lets the city affirmatively choose where capacity goes. But it doesn't reduce total capacity below SB 79's bar.

For the cleanest separation between C and D, see the FAQ entry on the rezone-at-50% option.

Which levers Council picked

Lever May 4 direction
A. Defaults + clarifying ordinance ✓ Yes. Staff to draft the clarifying ordinance.
B. Historic-property exemption ✓ Yes. Emergency ordinance to be brought back for adoption before July 1, 2026.
C. Rezone-to-50% delay trigger ✓ Yes. Staff to begin a rezoning effort across all three Caltrain station areas (University Avenue, California Avenue, San Antonio).
D. TOD Alternative Plan ◯ Open. Discussed but not committed to. Could be revisited as the rezoning work matures.

Translating: when the staff briefing said "a hybrid of two tracks," Track 1 was levers A + B (the near-term, ordinance-only moves) and Track 2 was lever C (the slower, rezoning-based delay). Lever D — the most ambitious option — was the road not yet taken.

The timeline now

  • May 4, 2026Council direction set: levers A + B + C, with D held in reserve.
  • May–June 2026Staff drafts the clarifying ordinance and the emergency historic-property exemption ordinance. Public hearing(s) before adoption.
  • By July 1, 2026If the historic-property ordinance is adopted in time, listed sites are exempt from SB 79 floors when the law goes operative. Otherwise, defaults apply to all sites.
  • July 1, 2026SB 79 §65912.157 takes effect statewide. In Palo Alto: 95 ft adjacent to stations, 75 ft within ¼ mile, 65 ft within ½ mile, by default.
  • 2026 → 2031Rezoning effort to deliver ≥50% of SB 79 capacity at all three Caltrain station areas. Coordinated with the San Antonio Road Area Plan and any restart of the Downtown Coordinated Area Plan.
  • January 31, 2032Outside deadline for cities to formally adopt local SB 79 ordinances per the statute.
  • ~2032If the 50% rezone is in place by the 7th-cycle housing element, SB 79 §65912.157 is deferred until one year after that cycle's adoption. If not, the full state floors apply by default.

What we're watching

Editorial — speculation, not reportage. What follows is our reading of how Council's May 4 direction could play out. None of this is a prediction or a city statement.

1. Reshape, not reduce

Council follows lever C with lever D. Capacity moves from the most contested streets (e.g. North Cal Ave, deep residential edges of the half-mile bands) onto better-suited parcels (commercial corridors, surface-parking lots, the Mollie Stone's-style sites). Total upzoning unchanged; spatial pattern transformed. Most aligned with a pro-housing reading of the statute.

2. Slow-walk to 2032

The rezoning takes longer than planned. The 50% trigger isn't pulled in time. SB 79's defaults apply ~2032 anyway, and the city has spent six years of staff capacity on a plan that didn't land. Worst-of-both: the shaping power gets traded for time, and then the time runs out.

3. Litigation or amendment

A charter-city lawsuit or a 2027–28 legislative amendment changes the framework before any of this matures. Past preemption challenges (SB 9, SB 35, the housing-element law) have largely lost in court — see the FAQ on court challenges — but the politics around SB 79 are more contested. Probability low; magnitude high.

The single most consequential variable: does the rezoning to ≥50% actually happen, on schedule, with real capacity at all three stations? If yes, Palo Alto buys time and shape. If no, the levers chosen on May 4 don't add up to much.
