SB 79 for laypeople

SB 79 is one of the most consequential housing laws California has passed in years. Here's what it actually does — without the legalese.

SB 79 in one sentence

Near major transit stops, California now sets minimum allowed building heights and densities — and local cities can't zone below those floors.

The tier system

SB 79 sorts transit stops into Tier 1 and Tier 2 based on how much service they get. The better the transit, the more housing the law unlocks nearby.

  • Tier 1: heavy rail or commuter rail with 72+ trains per day. Includes Caltrain (Palo Alto, Cal Ave, San Antonio), BART, and select LA Metro lines.
  • Tier 2: light rail, Bus Rapid Transit, or commuter rail with 48–71 trains per day. Includes SF Muni Metro, VTA, SacRT, San Diego Trolley, LA Metro light rail.

What the law actually requires near a Tier 1 station

Within a half-mile of a Tier 1 stop, local governments cannot block housing that meets these floors:

Distance from station Min allowed height Min allowed density About this many stories
Adjacent (within 200 ft) 95 ft 160 units / acre ~9–10
Within ¼ mile 75 ft 120 units / acre, FAR 3.5 ~7
Within ½ mile 65 ft 100 units / acre ~5–6

Buildings taller than 85 ft must meet SB 423 labor standards (skilled-and-trained workforce or prevailing wage). Cities can adopt a "TOD alternative plan" to reshape where the new capacity goes — but only if they deliver the same total zoned capacity overall.

What changed legally

Two things matter:

  • Preemption. Cities can no longer enforce zoning that's stricter than SB 79's floors near Tier 1 and Tier 2 stations. Local height caps, density caps, and FAR limits below the state floor are simply void inside the affected radius.
  • Ministerial approval. Qualifying projects can use the SB 35 path: staff-level approval, no discretionary review, no CEQA litigation. If the project meets the rules on paper, it gets approved on paper.

Timeline

  • January 2025SB 79 introduced by Sen. Scott Wiener.
  • Spring & summer 2025Multiple amendments through committee — affordability requirements, labor standards, and TOD alternative plan path added.
  • September 2025Passes both chambers.
  • October 10, 2025Signed into law by Gov. Gavin Newsom.
  • 2026 onwardCities adopt or contest. Palo Alto staff are exploring a TOD alternative plan.

Who supports it, who pushes back

Coalitions of housing advocates (California YIMBY), transit groups, environmental organizations, and the building trades pushed for SB 79 — arguing California needs millions more homes and that putting them near transit is the lowest-impact way to do it. Many cities and homeowner groups opposed it, citing local control, infrastructure capacity, and neighborhood character. The bill was significantly amended through 2025 to address objections without giving up the core preemption mechanism.
