With a new housing law scrambling Palo Alto’s plans for residential development, city leaders have opted to pause their effort to create a new vision for the downtown area.



Instead, city planners and members of the City Council plan to shift their focus to better understand the implications of Senate Bill 79, legislation that allows more height, unit density and building area at sites within half mile of transit stops, which in Palo Alto includes the Caltrain station at downtown, California Avenue and San Antonio Avenue.



Mayor Ed Lauing said last week that he will create a council committee that will work with staff to explore the impacts of SB 79 in each of the applicable areas and to consider the creation of an “alternative plan,” in accordance with the new legislation. Such a plan would give the city the flexibility to shift some development from the area immediately next to the station to other areas, provided the overall level of growth does not diminish.



“We feel like we really have to understand SB 79 for the entire city and then go back to the specifics for the Downtown Plan,” Lauing said at a press briefing on Nov. 6. “In essence, it would be too narrow to start looking at the Downtown Plan now, when we suddenly have a very high priority.”



He emphasized that the city is not dropping the Downtown Housing Plan, which is funded largely by a state grant and which aims to add housing to an area of the city that is roughly bounded by Alma Street on the west, Webster Street on east, Everett Avenue on the north and Forest Avenue on the south. The goal of the plan is to bring housing to an area that has seen very little residential growth in recent years and which was largely excluded from the city’s ambitious and state-mandated plan to add 6,086 housing units by 2031.




The city has already made some progress on the downtown plan, with the city dividing the area into four subareas and proposing a plan to focus height and density on the west side of the planning area, near the railroad tracks.



That decision became largely moot in September, when Gov. Gavin Newsom signed SB 79 into law, effectively granting developers the right to build housing projects with heights of up to 95 feet next to transit stops and with heights of 65 feet within ½ mile of transit. The bill, which was authored by state Sen. Scott Wiener, also allows expands density limits in transit-friendly zones. Projects will now be able to have between 100 and 160 units per acre, more than twice what the city’s underlying zoning currently allows.



“Staff anticipates that these legislative actions will result in significant physical changes to the City’s built environment over the next several years, particularly if market conditions improve,” a recent report from the Department of Planning and Development Services states.



City staff is still analyzing the potential impacts of SB 79 on local growth, Planning Director Jonathan Lait said at a recent discussion of the new bill and its implications for the downtown plan.



Following his presentation, the council agreed to pause the downtown effort so that staff and the council can focus on SB 79. While the bill allows significant height and density near the downtown transit stop, Lait suggested that an alternative plan would give the city the flexibility to shift some of that growth to other areas, where development could be both more suitable and more likely to occur. He noted at the Oct. 22 discussion that many of the sites in the downtown area are commercial and unlikely to be redeveloped.




“Office is still a more financially attractive option that housing and may not lend itself to development as much as other parts of the city, like California Avenue … on the east side of the train tracks,” Lait said.



The city is still moving ahead with a separate planning effort for the area around San Antonio Road, where the city hopes to add about 2,000 housing units over the next six years. Officials aren’t as alarmed about SB 79 scrambling the zoning rules in this area, given that the city has already relaxed height and density restrictions in this area to facilitate the new housing. Planning staff said they will proceed with the San Antonio plan and will integrate the results of its SB 79 analysis into the document.



The council agreed, however, that the downtown plan should take a break while the city considers the impacts of SB 79. Council member Pat Burt, who will serve on the new ad hoc committee, said he believes the community would benefit from seeing what the different parts of the city would potentially look like under the development standards of the new legislation.



“We have a prospect of thousands of new units in a concentrated area, in heights and FAR (floor-area-ratio) and unit density that we never envisioned, and with potentially tremendous impacts on traffic and infrastructure needs,” Burt said.
