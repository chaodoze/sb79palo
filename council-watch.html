SB 79 — Palo Alto's response

Status: Originally agendized May 4, 2026; deferred at 10:15 PM after earlier items ran long. City Manager floated May 18, 2026 as a possible reschedule (would require bumping another already-scheduled item).

What's at stake, simply

SB 79 is going to upzone Palo Alto. That is no longer a choice. What is a choice is whether Council does anything about it — and the asymmetry there is sharp.

Track 1 (the default): Council does nothing. On July 1, 2026, SB 79's full height/density floors (95 ft adjacent to stations, 75 ft within ¼ mile, 65 ft within ½ mile) apply automatically at every eligible site within half a mile of the three Caltrain stations. The state law is self-executing. No vote required, no staff work required, no city ordinance required. Track 1 happens by default if Council does nothing.

Track 2 (opt-in): Council has to actively choose this and direct staff to do months or years of work. Staff drafts an amendment to the housing element. Staff amends the General Plan and the zoning code. Public hearings. HCD reviews. Council votes to adopt. Council also adopts a separate ordinance pointing to the new zoning. Only after all of that is in place does §65912.161(b)(1) defer SB 79 to ~2032. Until the whole package is adopted, SB 79 applies by default.

Track 2 is not "the other option." Track 2 is "the option that requires Council to commit, fund, and complete a multi-year rezoning program." If Council does not direct that program, Track 1 is what happens.

Either way, by ~2032 SB 79 applies in full at all eligible sites within half a mile of the three Caltrain stations. The choice is about timing (now vs ~2032) and authorship (state-designed defaults vs city-designed half-strength rezoning) — not about whether the upzoning happens.

Independently of Track 1 vs Track 2, Council can also affirmatively direct other things — historic-property exemptions, upzoning of city-owned land, design standards, area plan updates. Each of those requires its own affirmative council action and its own staff work. None happen by default.

What "half-strength" actually means

The Track 2 rezone has to deliver at least 50% of SB 79's eligible capacity. Capacity is measured in total residential units or residential floor area across each station area — not in building heights at every individual site. Per city staff's pre-meeting briefing, the SB 79 defaults would unlock roughly:

Station areaSB 79 capacity (25-yr est.)Track 2 floor (≥50%)
Downtown / University Ave~1,600 units~800 units
California Avenue~900 units~450 units
San Antonio~200 units~100 units
City total (three stations)~2,700 units~1,350 units

Capacity estimates are 25-year baseline from staff; the ad-hoc committee assumes density bonuses could roughly double these in practice. Source: PA Online preview, April 27, 2026.

Because capacity is measured in aggregate, the city has discretion over where the units land:

  • Some parcels can be zoned at full SB 79 height (95 ft adjacent / 75 ft within ¼ mi / 65 ft within ½ mi) — e.g. commercial corridors, surface-parking lots.
  • Other parcels can be zoned closer to existing limits — e.g. residential edges of the half-mile bands.
  • As long as the aggregate at each station area clears the 50% floor, the trigger is met.

So "half-strength" is a citywide and per-station quantity floor, not a per-building height cap. Track 2 is best understood as: "the city writes its own zoning that allows at least ~1,350 new units across the three stations, distributed however the city wants, and SB 79's full defaults are deferred to ~2032."

The four legal levers (compact)

LeverWhat it doesAction required
A. SB 79 defaults (Track 1) SB 79 self-executes July 1, 2026. State height/density floors apply at every eligible site. Council can optionally pass a clarifying ordinance for procedural details, but the substantive floors apply with or without it. None. Happens automatically. The clarifying ordinance is housekeeping — not required for SB 79 to take effect.
B. Historic-property exemption Exempt local-register historic sites from SB 79 floors. Capped at 10% of the eligible area in any TOD zone. Council vote to adopt an ordinance, before July 1, 2026. If not adopted by that date, no historic protection on day one.
C. Rezone-to-50% delay (Track 2) Rezone the three station areas in the housing element to allow ≥50% of SB 79's total residential capacity (~1,350 units across all three stations, vs. SB 79's ~2,700). Distribution within the bands is up to the city. Once done, full SB 79 (§65912.157) is deferred to ~2032. Multi-year work program. Council direction → housing element amendment → General Plan / zoning code amendments → public hearings → HCD review → adoption → exempting ordinance. Until the entire package is adopted, SB 79 applies by default.
D. TOD Alternative Plan File a plan with HCD that redistributes capacity across the half-mile bands. Total citywide capacity must be maintained; per-zone reductions capped at 50%. The most comprehensive opt-in. Plan drafted, HCD reviewed and approved, adopted by Council. Longer effort than C; not yet directed.

A is the floor — whatever Council doesn't actively replace, A applies. B / C / D are stackable opt-ins; each requires its own affirmative council action and its own staff effort. Background — the four legal levers in full detail.

What we're watching at the next meeting

  • Does the historic-property exemption ordinance get formal direction to be brought back for adoption before July 1, 2026? Without that, it doesn't apply on day one.
  • Does Council give staff a real direction on the rezone-to-50% path (Track 2), or just an "explore" non-commitment?
  • Is the Alternative Plan path (lever D) opened or closed for future consideration?
  • What objective design standards does staff propose for SB 79 projects?
  • Does Council direct any affirmative upzoning of city-owned land, independent of either track?

How to engage

Council decisions get shaped by who shows up — in person, online, or in writing. Three ways in:

Live public comment

Council meets at City Hall (250 Hamilton Ave) and on Zoom. Each speaker gets up to 3 minutes (subject to the Mayor's discretion when many people sign up). Sign up before the item is called.

Meeting links & Zoom dial-in are on the agenda

Written comment

Email city.council@cityofpaloalto.org. Comments are entered into the public record and circulated to all council members. Send at least 24 hours before the meeting to ensure they're reviewed by the time of vote.

Track agendas

Council packets are posted ~11 days before each meeting on the city clerk page and on the PrimeGov portal. We list the SB 79-relevant ones above as they come up.

Community organizations active on Palo Alto housing: this list will be added as we identify the active ones in the SB 79 conversation. Tip: orgs you'd like us to track can be sent in — see about.

Why this page exists

Council decisions on SB 79 (and the wider housing conversation) move fast and aren't well-summarized in any one place. Staff reports run long; press coverage is uneven; agendas are written for procedural completeness, not for residents who want to know what's about to happen. This page is the plain-English, primary-source-grounded version. Each entry stays up until the decision lands; then it moves to a "past decisions" log (not yet present — nothing has resolved yet).
