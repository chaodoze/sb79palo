What SB 79 means for Palo Alto

Palo Alto has three Caltrain stations. Starting July 1, 2026, the new state minimums for taller, denser apartment buildings apply within a half-mile walk of each one. Here's exactly which blocks are affected, what's already getting proposed, and what the city is trying to do about it.

The three Palo Alto stations

Palo Alto / University Ave

The downtown station. Surrounded by University Ave retail, surface parking lots along Alma, and low-rise commercial — the parts most likely to redevelop first.

City staff estimate: ~1,600 new units over 25 years.

Top group · 104 trains/weekday (how we counted)

California Avenue

Restaurant row, two-story commercial, and adjacent residential blocks all fall inside the half-mile band.

City staff estimate: ~900 new units over 25 years.

Top group · 90 trains/weekday (how we counted)

San Antonio

Border with Mountain View. Mostly office and shopping (San Antonio Center, the old HP campus), so fewer parcels are easy to assemble for housing.

City staff estimate: ~200 new units over 25 years.

Top group · 90 trains/weekday (how we counted)

Together: about 2,700 new homes across Palo Alto's three station areas over 25 years — that's the city's own staff estimate.

All three Caltrain stations qualify in the top group. We pulled Caltrain's public schedule and counted weekday stops at each station: University Ave 104, California Ave 90, San Antonio 90 — all comfortably above the 72-train threshold. See the count →

What changes around each station, parcel by parcel

Each map below shades every Palo Alto parcel (lot) by how close it is to the train station — because closer parcels get bigger required apartment sizes. Deepest red is within 200 feet of the platform, where the minimum is 95 feet (about 9–10 stories). Mid-red is within a quarter-mile (minimum 75 feet, about 7 stories). The lighter wash is the rest of the half-mile area (minimum 65 feet, about 5–6 stories). What changes downtown

Most of downtown Palo Alto caps buildings at 50 feet today. Inside a quarter-mile of the University Ave Caltrain station, the new minimum becomes 75 feet (about 7 stories) — and 95 feet (about 9–10 stories) on the parcels right next to the station.

The parcels most likely to redevelop first are the ones easiest to combine and lowest in current value per square foot: surface parking lots along Alma, single-story commercial buildings along University, and aging two-story offices along Lytton and Hamilton.

Specific projects in motion

SB 79 doesn't kick in until July 1, 2026, so there are no SB-79-specific projects yet. But four threads are already shaping what's coming:

Redco — 156 California Ave

382 apartments across three buildings (17, 11, 7 stories) on the Mollie Stone's site at Cal Ave. Filed in late 2023 using a different California state law (called the builder's remedy, which lets developers bypass local zoning when a city's housing plan is out of compliance), not SB 79. Environmental review was drafted December 2024; status pending. Mollie Stone's would be preserved on the ground floor.

Why it matters here: it's the most concrete picture of what an SB-79-sized project at Cal Ave would look like. Coverage

Downtown vision plan — paused

The city's multi-year project to write a new vision for downtown (officially the "Downtown Coordinated Area Plan") was paused in November 2025 so staff could refocus on SB 79. Mayor Lauing put together a Council subcommittee to evaluate options. The downtown plan may pick back up, but as part of the SB 79 response rather than as its own separate effort.

Coverage

San Antonio area plan

A 275-acre planning project for the corridor around the San Antonio Caltrain station. The draft circulated in September 2025. It now overlaps with the SB 79 area around San Antonio — and will need to coordinate with whatever Palo Alto eventually adopts as its SB 79 response.

How the city is responding, in one paragraph

SB 79 is going to allow taller, denser apartment buildings around the three Caltrain stations. If Council does nothing, the new state minimums kick in automatically on July 1, 2026. If Council wants to push back, the only real option is for the city to write its own zoning that allows at least half as many new homes (~1,350 across the three stations) and submit it to the state — that's a years-long process, but it would push the full state minimums out to ~2032. Separately, Council can also vote to exempt historic properties, allow taller buildings on city-owned land, and write design rules — each of those is a separate Council vote.
