FAQ

Common questions and pushback

These are real concerns neighbors raise. Short, sourced answers — not dismissals.

Won't this cause more traffic?

Probably less than people fear, especially near transit. Studies of transit-oriented development consistently find that residents of housing within a half-mile of frequent rail drive significantly fewer miles per day than residents elsewhere — often 30–50% fewer. Caltrain electrification (now running) makes that effect stronger by improving service frequency.

That said, peak-hour congestion at intersections like Alma & University will still be real. Traffic studies and complete-streets investments are reasonable demands.

What about parking? Will my street fill up?

SB 79 reduces or removes parking minimums on qualifying projects, but most large developers near transit still build some parking because the market expects it. Cities can keep on-street residential permit programs, which limit overflow from new buildings into adjacent neighborhoods.

Will Palo Alto's schools be overwhelmed?

2,700 net new units over 25 years is roughly 100 new homes a year — well below historical PAUSD enrollment swings. Multifamily housing produces fewer school-age children per unit than single-family on average. Districts adjust class sizes and boundaries year to year as a normal part of operations.

What about the character of the neighborhood?

SB 79 only affects parcels within a half-mile of Tier 1 transit. In Palo Alto that means downtown, Cal Ave, and San Antonio — all already commercial or mixed-use cores. Single-family neighborhoods like Old Palo Alto, Crescent Park, Professorville, and most of Midtown sit outside the affected radius.

Why override local control?

California's housing shortage is a statewide problem with statewide consequences (homelessness, displacement, out-migration). The legislature concluded — across many bills, not just SB 79 — that letting each city veto housing has produced a multi-decade shortfall, and that some baseline floor is needed near transit specifically because that's where new housing has the lowest infrastructure and emissions cost.

Cities that prefer to shape rather than block growth still have the TOD alternative plan path.

Will SB 79 actually produce affordable housing?

Mixed answer. SB 79 includes affordability requirements and labor standards for taller buildings, and projects on public-transit-agency land have stronger affordability rules. But most market-rate housing built under SB 79 will not be income-restricted. The argument is that adding supply (any supply) reduces price pressure across the market, and that dedicated subsidies are still needed for deeply affordable units.

What if a developer ignores design or quality standards?

Cities keep their objective design standards — setbacks, materials, ground-floor activation — as long as those don't effectively block the height/density floors. Ministerial approval means staff check the project against those objective rules; it doesn't mean anything goes.

Does this affect my single-family home?

Not directly — SB 79 is about what's permitted on parcels near transit. It doesn't force anyone to sell or rebuild. Property owners choose if and when to redevelop. Most single-family parcels near downtown are also not easy to assemble into the kind of larger sites SB 79 buildings need.
