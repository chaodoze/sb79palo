Local impact

What SB 79 means for Palo Alto

Palo Alto has three Caltrain stations — and Caltrain is Tier 1. SB 79's strongest height and density allowances apply within a half-mile of each one. Here's the rough picture.

The three Palo Alto stations

Palo Alto (University Ave) Caltrain

Tier 1. The downtown station. Surrounded by University Avenue retail, surface parking lots, and low-rise commercial — the parts of downtown most likely to redevelop first.

City staff estimate: ~1,600 new units over 25 years.

California Avenue Caltrain

Tier 1. Restaurant row, two-story commercial, and adjacent residential blocks all fall inside the half-mile band.

City staff estimate: ~900 new units over 25 years.

San Antonio Caltrain

Tier 1. Border with Mountain View. Surrounded by office and shopping rather than residential, so impact is smaller.

City staff estimate: ~200 new units over 25 years.

Together: about 2,700 new homes across Palo Alto's three station areas over a 25-year horizon — and that's the city's own estimate, not an advocacy group's.

What changes downtown

Most of downtown Palo Alto today is capped at 50 feet by local zoning. Inside a quarter-mile of University Avenue Caltrain, SB 79 raises the floor to 75 feet (about 7 stories) — and 95 feet (about 9–10 stories) right next to the station.

The properties most likely to redevelop are the ones easiest to assemble and lowest in current value-per-square-foot: surface parking lots along Alma, single-story commercial along University, and aging two-story office buildings.

How the city is responding

Palo Alto staff have been preparing rezoning options and exploring a TOD alternative plan — a path SB 79 leaves open for cities that want to direct the new capacity to specific sites, as long as the total zoned capacity citywide is at least as much as SB 79 would have delivered.

Whether that path actually reduces growth, or just reshapes where it happens, is one of the most consequential local debates of the next two years.
