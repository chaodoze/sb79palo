SB 79 — Palo Alto's response

Status: Originally agendized May 4, 2026; deferred at 10:15 PM after earlier items ran long. City Manager floated May 18, 2026 as a possible reschedule (would require bumping another already-scheduled item).

What's at stake, simply

SB 79 is going to allow taller, denser apartment buildings around Palo Alto's three Caltrain stations. That part isn't up for debate. The choice Council faces is whether to do anything about it — and the difference between those choices is bigger than it looks.

If Council does nothing, the new state minimums kick in on their own on July 1, 2026 — 95 feet (about 9–10 stories) right next to each station, 75 feet (about 7 stories) within a quarter-mile walk, 65 feet (about 5–6 stories) within a half-mile walk. That happens automatically. No Council vote, no city law, no staff work needed.

If Council wants to push back, the only real option is for the city to write its own zoning that allows at least half as many new homes as SB 79 would (roughly 1,350 across the three stations), get the state's housing department to sign off, and pass a city law pointing at the new zoning. That whole package takes months or years. Until it's done, the state minimums apply automatically.

The "do its own zoning" path is not "the other option you can pick." It's "the option that requires Council to commit to a multi-year project and finish it." If Council doesn't direct that project, the state minimums kick in by default.

Either way, by around 2032 SB 79 applies in full at all qualifying sites near the three Caltrain stations. The choice is about when (now vs ~2032) and who designs it (the state, or the city writing its own version) — not about whether the bigger apartment buildings happen.

Separately, Council can also vote to do other things: exempt historic properties, allow taller buildings on city-owned land, write design rules, update area plans. Each of those needs its own Council vote and its own staff work. None happen by default.

What "half as many homes" actually means

If Palo Alto wants to use the city-zoning path, it has to allow at least half as many new homes as SB 79 would — counted across all three station areas combined, not just at any one station. Here's what city staff estimates SB 79 would allow:

Station areaIf SB 79 applies (25-yr est.)If city writes its own zoning (minimum)
Downtown / University Ave~1,600 homes~800 homes
California Avenue~900 homes~450 homes
San Antonio~200 homes~100 homes
All three stations~2,700 homes~1,350 homes

Estimates are city staff's 25-year projection; the Council ad-hoc committee thinks the actual numbers could roughly double once you factor in state density bonuses. Source: PA Online preview, April 27, 2026.

The "at least half as many homes" rule is counted as a total, not a building-by-building cap. So the city has flexibility on where those homes go:

  • Some lots can be zoned at the full SB 79 size (95 feet right next to a station, 75 feet within a quarter-mile, 65 feet within a half-mile) — for example commercial corridors, surface-parking lots.
  • Other lots can be left closer to today's limits — for example residential streets at the edges of the half-mile area.
  • As long as each station area's total still adds up to at least half of SB 79's total, the city qualifies for the delay.

So the city-zoning path is best understood as: "the city writes zoning that allows at least about 1,350 new homes across the three stations, decides where those go, and in exchange the full SB 79 minimums don't kick in until around 2032."

The four ways Council can respond

OptionWhat it doesWhat Council has to do
A. State minimums kick in automatically On July 1, 2026, the state's required minimum apartment sizes apply at every qualifying site near the three stations. Council can pass a city law about details (parking, design review, the approval process) but the size minimums apply either way. Nothing. Happens by default. The detail-level city law is optional housekeeping.
B. Exempt historic properties Pull listed historic sites out of the new state minimums. Limit: at most 10% of the affected area in each station's half-mile zone can be exempt this way. A Council vote on a city law, before July 1, 2026. If not passed by then, historic sites aren't protected on day one.
C. City writes its own zoning that delays the state minimums The city rezones around the three stations to allow at least 1,350 new homes total (about half of what SB 79 would). The city decides where those homes go. Once it's done, the full state minimums are pushed back to about 2032. A multi-year project. Council direction → update Palo Alto's official housing plan → rewrite the General Plan and zoning code → public hearings → state housing-department sign-off → pass the new zoning → pass a city law pointing at the new zoning. Until that whole package is done, the state minimums still apply.
D. City submits a full alternative plan to the state A more ambitious version of C: the city writes a plan that redistributes where the new homes go around the stations. The state has to approve it. Total homes still have to add up to what SB 79 requires; any single station's area can be cut by at most 50%. The biggest project of all four. The plan has to be drafted, reviewed and approved by the state, then passed by Council. Even longer effort than C; not yet directed.

A is what happens by default. B, C, and D are each separate things Council can choose to do on top of (or instead of) A — and each requires its own vote and its own staff project. They stack: Council can pick more than one. Background — the four options in full detail.

What to watch for at the next meeting

  • Does Council vote to bring back the historic-property exemption city law for actual adoption before July 1, 2026? Without that vote, historic sites aren't protected on day one.
  • Does Council actually direct staff to begin the city-zoning project, or just say "explore the option" (which doesn't commit to anything)?
  • Is the full alternative-plan-to-the-state path left open as a future option, or taken off the table?
  • What design rules (about ground-floor uses, materials, setbacks) does staff propose for the new buildings?
  • Does Council direct any taller buildings on city-owned land — separate from any of the SB 79 options?

How to engage

Council decisions get shaped by who shows up — in person, online, or in writing. Three ways in:

Live public comment

Council meets at City Hall (250 Hamilton Ave) and on Zoom. Each speaker gets up to 3 minutes (subject to the Mayor's discretion when many people sign up). Sign up before the item is called.

Meeting links & Zoom dial-in are on the agenda

Written comment

Email city.council@cityofpaloalto.org. Comments are entered into the public record and circulated to all council members. Send at least 24 hours before the meeting to ensure they're reviewed by the time of vote.

Track agendas

Council packets are posted ~11 days before each meeting on the city clerk page and on the PrimeGov portal. We list the SB 79-relevant ones above as they come up.

Community organizations active on Palo Alto housing: this list will be added as we identify the active ones in the SB 79 conversation. Tip: orgs you'd like us to track can be sent in — see about.

Why this page exists

Council decisions on SB 79 (and the wider housing conversation) move fast and aren't well-summarized in any one place. Staff reports run long; press coverage is uneven; agendas are written for procedural completeness, not for residents who want to know what's about to happen. This page is the plain-English, primary-source-grounded version. Each entry stays up until the decision lands; then it moves to a "past decisions" log (not yet present — nothing has resolved yet).
